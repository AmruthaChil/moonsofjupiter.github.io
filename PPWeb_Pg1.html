Jupiter

  1. Introduction

    1. It is the fifth planet from the Sun.
    2. It is fittingly named after Jupiter, the Supreme God since it is the supreme planet of our solar system in the aspects of both Mass and size.
    3. Jupiter, despite being extremely massive in mass and size, rotates extremely fast on its axis.
    4. The mass of Jupiter is twice that of the rest of the solar system combined, except the Sun.

    5. The pressure in Jupiter starts increasing as the depth increases.

    6. According to Dr Pamela Gay, “A planet the size of Jupiter is not really possible, in terms of how long it takes to move around its equator.

    7. Jupiter emits more heat than it releases.

    8. It also has a ring, but it is hardly visible from Earth and was only discovered when probes were sent to Jupiter.

  2. Features:

    1. The most popular feature about Jupiter that many people know of, is its Great Red Spot.
    2. The great red spot is the largest storm in Jupiter. It has been raging for ages, and can even be seen from the Earth.

    3. It is a gas planet, which means that it does not have a solid surface.
    4. Gas on the surface becomes thicker and hotter deeper down Jupiter and eventually turns into a liquid due to the great pressure and temperatures.
    5. Jupiter may not have a solid core below this either.

    6. It is thought to have a core of liquid metallic hydrogen, which is hotter than the Sun!
    7. In liquid metallic hydrogen, individual atoms share their electrons instead of holding on to them.
    8. This transformation is as a result of extremely high pressures at that depth of Jupiter.
    9. The liquid metallic hydrogen can now display many properties of metals.


    10. Its atmosphere is about 70.8 km deep.
    11. The air of Jupiter consists of a variety of gases like:
      1. Hydrogen
      2. Helium
      3. Ammonia
      4. Methane
      5. Many other poisonous gases

  3. Surface:

    1. There are “stripes” of different colours that we can see on the surface of Jupiter - shades of red, white, yellow and brown.
    2. These are storms, and the colours we see are the surfaces of the clouds of Jupiter.


    3. The lighter coloured stripes are called zones.
    4. The darker coloured stripes are called belts.
    5. They go around Jupiter in opposite directions.
    6. These have formed due to convection in Jupiter’s atmosphere.

    7. Zones are generated when ammonia in the air cools moves upwards.
    8. Belts are formed when this same air moves towards the sides and sinks.
    9. The chemistry of these clouds is changed due to sunlight, and the molecules turn into darker colours like red and brown.


    It has an enormous magnetic field, and it is thought that this is the reason it has as many as 79 moons.
